A free online project where anyone can explore a map of the real stars near us, study real data from NASA's planet-hunting telescopes, and help decide which distant worlds are the best places to look for life. Built by the Griswold family as a for-fun, for-real science project.

How can a game help find life?

Computers are fast, but people are better at spotting the unexpected. Planet-hunting telescopes produce enormous streams of data — mostly a simple measurement of how bright a star is, minute by minute. When a planet passes in front of its star, the star dims by a whisper. Software flags many of these dips automatically, but it misses real ones and trips over fakes. Human eyes catch what the software misses — volunteers on earlier projects have even helped discover real planets this way. When you play, you become a second set of eyes on real telescope data.

What you actually do

You're shown a light curve — a wiggly line of a star's brightness over time — and you decide whether a small repeating dip is a real planet crossing the star, or just noise, a glitch, or two stars eclipsing each other. It takes seconds. And no single person's answer decides anything: every piece of data is shown to many people, and the project trusts the agreement of the crowd, not any one click.

What we're really trying to prove

Not "is there life out there, yes or no" — that's too big for any one project. We chase small, answerable questions that stack up: for each planet, is this a real world or a false alarm? For each star we listen to, is there a signal we can't explain as Earth-made interference? Every confident answer sharpens the map of the best places to point the world's biggest telescopes next.

How we know it's working

Mixed into the real data are cards where we already know the answer, so we can measure how accurate each player is. Because many people see the same data, we can measure how much they agree — and only strong agreement moves forward. We even hide artificial planet signals to check what fraction the crowd catches. We publish those numbers, so you can see it working.

The honest part

We will almost certainly not find aliens — nearly every promising signal in history has turned out to be interference or a natural quirk. We think that's worth saying plainly. What we can really do is valuable: help confirm new planets, rule stars in and out, sharpen the list of the best places to look, and maybe help co-discover a genuine new world.

Frequently asked questions

Is this a game or real science?

Both, on purpose. The exploring, charting, and naming are the fun wrapper; underneath, you're doing a real task scientists need. The fun is attached to genuine work, not a substitute for it.

Do I need an account? Is it safe for kids?

You can play with just a name and an avatar — no email required. It's built to be family-safe: we collect almost nothing, we don't ask children for personal information, and public names are moderated. Welcoming for families, classrooms, and scout troops.

Can I really name a planet?

You can give an honorary nickname to a world you helped chart, and it lives on our map as a thank-you. To be clear, that's a celebratory nickname, not an official name — official naming is handled by the International Astronomical Union.

Is it free? What data do you use?

Yes, it's free. It runs on free, public data: confirmed-planet data from the NASA Exoplanet Archive, and — for the radio-astronomy phase coming later — the Breakthrough Listen open data initiative.

Who's behind it?

The Griswold family in Atlanta — the folks behind Pixie Vacations and the Mouse Chat podcast. This is their passion project: use free public data and a lot of curious people to chase a big question, and have fun doing it.

What's next?

A second phase adds radio-astronomy: instead of light curves, you'll help tell a genuine signal from a distant star apart from everyday Earth interference. Same idea, new kind of data.
